Kaelen had spent his life stealing from the dead because the living had never left him much worth keeping.

He knew the old rules of ruin-work: trust the floor less than the walls, trust the walls less than silence, and never trust a tomb that still had birds nesting outside it. Birds knew where death slept. They avoided places that had begun to dream.

But hunger was a simpler curse than fear.

For three weeks, Kaelen had followed a trail of broken maps, dead informants, and half-burned priestly records across the drowned lowlands of Veyr. Each scrap told the same story in a different language: before the marsh swallowed the southern kingdom, there had been a monarch who called himself the Sun Made Flesh. He built a tomb beneath his palace before the floodwaters came, sealed himself inside with his crown, his guards, his treasures, and the priests who knew how to bind sunlight into stone.

No one agreed on whether the king had died.

That was the part Kaelen disliked.

The last man who spoke of the tomb had done so with blackened lips and a fevered grin. He claimed the Golden Crown still rested below, untouched, worth enough to buy a province or a pardon. He also claimed the tomb had rules of its own: metal whispered, bones listened, and anything blessed by ancient light would glow when danger came near.

Kaelen had laughed then. Quietly. Politely.

He did not laugh when the dying man seized his wrist and pressed a cracked clay token into his palm.

"Do not fight the tomb", the man rasped. "The strong are buried deepest. The clever sometimes leave."

Now, at twilight, Kaelen stood before the drowned king’s outer ruins, his dagger rusted, his pack empty, his rope coiled over one shoulder, and an unlit torch in hand.

Behind him lay debt, pursuit, and starvation.

Ahead lay the Tomb of the Sunken King.

And somewhere beneath its stones, if the legends had not lied, waited a crown bright enough to change his fate.
